SECTION 1: Identity & Beliefs

1. What's one core belief you hold about life, success, or people that most others in your world would push back on?

I believe that anything is possible for anyone - and I mean anyone, not just the people who got dealt a decent hand. The lad on the council estate with nothing in the fridge. The woman working three jobs who hasn't slept properly in two years. The bloke who got told he was thick his entire childhood. When you're willing to be a good person, when you're genuine, and when you do things for the right reasons, doors open that shouldn't logically open. I don't believe in victim mindsets. I grew up on Base Green - I rode the special bus to school every single day past kids who had normal lives, normal families, normal brains. I could've made that my identity. I could've worn it like armour and used it as a reason to never try. But feeling sorry for yourself is just renting a room in a burning building and refusing to leave because you've got the curtains how you like them. I've got zero tolerance for people who marinate in their own excuses when they've got two hands and a heartbeat.

What life experience formed this belief — and when did you first realise it was yours?

Uganda, 2008. I was sixteen years old standing in a village watching a blind musician play like his hands were connected to something the rest of us couldn't hear. This man had nothing - no stage, no audience, no Spotify profile, no functioning eyes - and he played with more joy and purpose than anyone I'd ever seen back home. The dust was thick, the heat was suffocating, and I'm stood there as a teenager from a Sheffield council estate with my jaw on the floor. I looked around at people who had a fraction of what we had back in England and they were building, creating, laughing, getting on with it. Not one of them was sat around waiting for permission or pity. That's when I knew - the belief that your circumstances define your ceiling is the biggest lie anyone ever sells you. I came home on that plane with something burning in my chest that hasn't gone out since.

How has holding this belief cost you something (a relationship, an opportunity, belonging)?

Sometimes this means people don't like the way I go about things. I'm direct. I'm straight to the point. I'd rather sit across from someone and tell them the honest, uncomfortable truth that actually gets to the root cause than spend forty-five minutes circling around the point like we're doing laps at a roundabout. I've lost friendships over it. I've had people leave discovery calls looking like I just told them their dog died. I've watched potential clients choose the coach who told them what they wanted to hear instead of what they needed to hear. There's a cost to honesty - people don't always want the mirror held up, especially when they've spent years avoiding their own reflection. I've had family members distance themselves because I refuse to play the game of pretending everything's fine when the house is on fire. But I'd rather have five people in my life who trust me completely than fifty who only stick around because I tell them they're brilliant when they're not.

How does this belief show up in the way you coach, lead, or create?

Some people will love who I am and how I am and what I do. Others, it's not the right fit - and that's absolutely fine. I'm not trying to be everyone's cup of tea. I'd rather be a strong black coffee that wakes you up than a lukewarm drink that sits there doing nothing. In my coaching, this means I won't let you hide behind busy. I won't congratulate you for working fourteen-hour days when your business has no systems and your family can't remember what your face looks like at dinner time. In my content, I talk about the bits most people in this industry polish over - the breakdowns, the bad decisions, the moments where I wanted to quit and probably should have. Because the people I'm here to serve don't need another highlight reel. They need someone who's been in the mud and can show them the exact route out.

2. What did you used to believe about yourself that you've had to completely dismantle?

I used to believe something was fundamentally broken inside me. Not just different - broken. ADHD and ASD went undiagnosed until my late twenties, which means I spent the best part of two decades trying to operate in a world that wasn't built for my brain and blaming myself every time I couldn't keep up. I thought I was lazy. I thought I was stupid. I thought the reason I couldn't sit still in a classroom or follow a conversation without my mind firing off in nine directions was because I wasn't trying hard enough. I also believed the only way to get anywhere was to just grind harder - wake up earlier, stay up later, work until your eyes bled. That's not the case. You need work ethic to get off the ground, absolutely, but the thing that actually moves a business is working smart. Breaking things down into processes, into SOPs, making sure you've got the right frameworks and structures underneath you. I spent years building on sand because I thought effort alone was the foundation.

Where did that belief originally come from — a person, a moment, a pattern?

It came from everywhere, constantly, like background noise you stop noticing because it's always been there. It started at home - growing up in a house where alcohol was the escape route and dysfunction was the wallpaper. Nobody sat me down and said "you're broken," but when you're a kid watching the adults around you numb themselves just to get through Tuesday, you absorb this unspoken message that life is something you survive, not something you shape. Then school reinforced it. Every morning I got on that bus - the special bus from Base Green to Dronfield - and every morning I watched the other kids at the normal stops with their normal bags and their normal mates. My brother was good at rugby. People knew his name for the right reasons. I was the one who froze under pressure and couldn't explain why his brain worked like a browser with forty-seven tabs open. The pattern was relentless - home said "cope," school said "conform," sport said "perform" - and I couldn't do any of them the way everyone else seemed to manage effortlessly.

What was the turning point where you stopped accepting it as truth?

The turning point was coming back from Uganda at sixteen. I remember landing at the airport and everything looking different, even though nothing had physically changed. I'd just watched people with genuinely nothing - no clean water, no healthcare, no safety net - build lives with more intention and joy than most people I knew back home. That blind musician was still playing in my head. I thought, right - if that man can create something beautiful with what he's got, what's my excuse? I'm a kid from Sheffield with all four limbs and a country that at least gives you a fighting chance. I made a decision on that plane that I was going to make something of myself and I was not going to stop until I got there. It wasn't a soft motivational moment. It was a line in the sand drawn with anger and fire and the absolute refusal to let Base Green be the whole story.

How does breaking that belief now influence the work you do with others?

Breaking that belief pushes me to want more from people than they want from themselves - and honestly, it's often the biggest catalyst for change I see. When someone realises that another person genuinely believes in who they are, what they're about, and what they're trying to build, it gives them this momentum and energy to push forward that they couldn't generate alone. It's like trying to push-start a car by yourself versus having someone shoulder-to-shoulder with you. I always say to people - I've not got the answers for absolutely everything, but I won't stop until we get to that point. Because I know what it feels like to sit in a room believing you're defective. I know what it costs you. I know how many years you can lose living inside that lie. So when I see someone operating from that same place of "I'm not good enough" or "my brain doesn't work right" or "people like me don't build things like this" - I'm not having it. I'll drag that belief out into the light and show them exactly why it's wrong, because someone should've done that for me twenty years earlier.

3. What's the most important lesson you've ever learned — and what did it cost you to learn it?

The most important lesson I ever learned was focusing on what actually matters - and stripping away everything that just looks like it matters. There's a massive difference between being busy and being productive, between growing a business and growing your ego, between building something that works and building something that just looks good on Instagram. I learned that the foundations come before the decoration. You don't tile the bathroom before the plumbing's in. You don't pick curtains when the walls haven't been built yet. But that's exactly what I did for years - I was decorating a house with no foundations and wondering why it kept falling down.

Walk through the moment or period in your life when this lesson landed.

2018. My meal prep business had gone from eight grand a month to sixty grand a month and I thought I was untouchable. I had staff, I had customers, I had revenue coming in that made my mates' jaws drop. But underneath it all, there was nothing - no systems, no processes, no structure. It was held together with duct tape, adrenaline, and my ability to outwork the chaos. Then it collapsed. Not slowly - it caved in like a building with rotten support beams. I lost the business. I'd already thrown thirty grand at a marketing company that delivered absolutely nothing. I went from sixty grand months to pot washing in a kitchen just to keep the lights on. I remember standing at that sink, hands red raw from hot water, and the silence in my head was deafening. No phone ringing, no orders coming in, no staff asking questions. Just me, a pile of dirty pans, and the full weight of every shortcut I'd taken sitting on my chest.

What would you have done differently if you'd known it sooner?

I would have built the boring stuff first. The SOPs, the systems, the frameworks, the processes that nobody posts about on social media because they're not sexy. I would have spent the first six months of that business building the engine instead of painting the bodywork. I would have asked for help earlier instead of assuming that working harder was always the answer. I would have hired based on structure, not desperation. I would have looked at the actual numbers instead of the vanity metrics that made me feel successful. But honestly - and this is the bit that people don't want to hear - I probably wouldn't have listened. I was twenty-something, making more money than anyone in my family had ever seen, and I was convinced I'd figured it out. Sometimes the lesson can only land after the building's already collapsed and you're standing in the rubble.

How do you now pass this lesson on — in your content, your coaching, or your conversations?

Every single thing I teach is built on this lesson. When I talk about SmartScaling, when I talk about AI-powered systems, when I talk about the 50/50 Rule - it all comes back to the same truth: build the foundations before you build the penthouse. In coaching calls, I'm the person who'll stop someone mid-sentence when they're excited about their new funnel or their new offer and ask them, "right, but what's the system underneath this? What happens when you get fifty clients and your current setup can't handle ten?" In my content, I show the pot washing. I show the crash. I show what happens when you scale without structure. Because the summit photo doesn't show you the blisters, and most people in this industry only post the summit photo. I'd rather be the person who shows you the whole climb - the wrong turns, the falls, the moments you wanted to turn back - so you can actually make it to the top without the same scars.

4. What belief do you hold about your clients or your audience that others in your industry might dismiss or overlook?

I believe that most business owners aren't struggling because they're lazy or stupid or lack ambition. They're struggling because they're doing everything themselves and nobody's ever shown them there's another way. The industry loves to frame it as a mindset problem - "you just need to want it more, wake up earlier, hustle harder." That's rubbish. I've worked with people who have more drive in their little finger than most gurus have in their entire content calendar. The problem isn't motivation. The problem is they're trying to be the chef, the waiter, the dishwasher, the accountant, and the marketing department all at the same time - and then someone on LinkedIn tells them they should also be posting three reels a day. They don't need another motivational quote. They need someone to walk in, look at the mess, and say "right, here's how we sort this out."

What experience gave you this perspective on the people you serve?

Running my own meal prep business into the ground because I tried to do every single thing myself. I was the one cooking, delivering, doing the accounts, answering customer messages at midnight, designing the menu, cleaning the kitchen. I was the entire operation, and I wore that like a badge of honour until it buried me. When I started working with business owners after rebuilding, I saw the exact same pattern in nearly every single one of them. That glazed look in their eyes. That nervous laugh when you ask them about their systems. The way they say "yeah, it's just me at the moment" like it's temporary when they've been saying it for three years. I recognised it because I'd lived it. They weren't failing - they were drowning in a business that had outgrown their capacity to run it alone.

How does this belief change the way you approach your work compared to others?

Most people in this space sell the dream and leave you to figure out the mechanics. I sell the mechanics because I know the dream takes care of itself when the engine actually works. I don't start with "what's your vision" - I start with "show me your week, show me where your time goes, show me what you're doing that a system could do better." It's not glamorous. It's not the kind of coaching that looks good in a testimonial video with cinematic music. But it's the difference between giving someone a fish and teaching them to set up a fishing operation with automation, quality control, and a distribution system. I build the infrastructure that means they can actually enjoy the business they started because they wanted freedom, not a second prison sentence with better branding.

What would you say directly to a client right now who needed to hear this belief?

You're not broken and you're not behind. You're just trying to carry the entire building on your back when you should be designing the structure that holds the weight for you. The fact that you've got this far doing everything yourself tells me you've got more capability than most people in any room you walk into. But capability without systems is just a fast road to burnout. Stop trying to be the hardest worker in the room and start being the smartest operator. I'm not here to fix you - there's nothing wrong with you. I'm here to build the machine around you so your talent actually gets to breathe.

5. What is something you are completely unapologetic about — in how you live, lead, or operate?

I am completely unapologetic about being direct. I will tell you exactly what I see, exactly what I think, and exactly what needs to happen - even if it makes the room uncomfortable. I don't wrap feedback in cotton wool. I don't sandwich criticism between compliments like some corporate training manual taught me. If your offer is weak, I'll say it's weak. If your content is boring, I'll tell you it's boring. If you're lying to yourself about why your business isn't growing, I'll point at the lie and name it. I grew up in a household where nobody said the real thing - everyone danced around the elephant in the room until the elephant crushed them. I decided a long time ago that I'd rather be the person who says the uncomfortable truth than the person who watches someone walk off a cliff with a smile and a thumbs up.

Was there ever a time you apologised for this, or tried to dim it down to fit in?

For years. Through school, through early business, through friendships where I'd water myself down because being "too much" wasn't socially acceptable. I'd bite my tongue in meetings. I'd nod along with strategies I knew were terrible. I'd watch people make decisions that I could see would blow up in their face and I'd say nothing because I didn't want to be "that guy." The worst version of it was in my early twenties when I was so desperate to be liked and accepted that I became a chameleon - shifting my personality to match whoever I was in the room with. I lost myself completely. I was agreeable, palatable, forgettable. And it nearly cost me everything because I was so busy being what everyone else wanted that I forgot to build what I actually needed.

What happened when you finally stopped hiding or softening it?

Some people left. Let's not romanticise it - being authentically direct costs you relationships. People I thought were close suddenly weren't comfortable anymore. But the people who stayed got closer. The clients who chose to work with me got better results because I wasn't wasting their time or their money being polite about problems that needed surgery, not a plaster. My business grew because my reputation became clear - you come to Ben if you want the truth, you go somewhere else if you want to feel good about staying stuck. The moment I stopped dimming it down was the same moment my brand started actually meaning something. Because people can feel authenticity. It's like the difference between a real fire and a screen saver of a fire - one of them actually keeps you warm.

How does this "unapologetic" quality show up as a strength in your brand or business?

It's the filter that means every person who works with me actually wants to change. The directness repels the people who just want validation and attracts the people who are serious about building something real. It means my coaching calls move fast - we don't spend twenty minutes warming up and making each other feel comfortable. We get in, we find the problem, we build the solution. My content hits differently because people can tell I'm not performing - I'm talking. Every caption, every video, every email comes from the same place: here's what's actually true, here's what actually works, and here's what you need to do about it. In an industry full of people saying what the algorithm wants to hear, being unapologetically honest is the most radical thing you can do.

SECTION 2: Defining Moments & Real Stories

6. What's the single most defining moment of your life — and how did it reshape who you are?

Standing in a village in Uganda at sixteen years old, watching a blind man play music that made every hair on my body stand up. That moment divided my life into before and after. Before Uganda, I was a kid from Base Green who rode the special bus and assumed the ceiling was low. After Uganda, I was someone who'd seen proof - real, undeniable, in-the-flesh proof - that your circumstances don't get to write the final chapter. That musician couldn't see the sky, couldn't see the people listening, couldn't see his own hands moving across the instrument - and he played like none of that mattered because it didn't.

Describe the moment in as much sensory detail as you can — where were you, who was there, what were you feeling?

The heat was the first thing - this thick, heavy warmth that sat on your skin like a second layer. Red dust everywhere, the kind that gets in your teeth and turns your trainers orange. I was stood in a loose circle of people, mostly locals, a few of us from the trip looking wide-eyed at everything because nothing in Sheffield prepares you for it. The musician was sat on a low wooden stool, his eyes closed or unfocused - I couldn't tell which - and his fingers were moving with this certainty that I'd never seen in anyone back home. The sound was raw, unpolished, nothing like the music I knew. It wasn't performed. It just came out of him like breathing. I remember my throat tightening. I remember this weird sensation of feeling simultaneously tiny and massive - tiny because of how much world existed that I'd never seen, massive because something inside me was expanding in real time. I felt angry and grateful and sad and fired up all at once. The smell of cooking from somewhere nearby, kids running past laughing, and this man just playing, just existing fully in what he was doing. No audience, no applause, no metric to measure it. Just pure, undiluted purpose.

Who were you before this moment, and who did you become after?

Before that moment I was a kid collecting evidence for why life was going to be hard. Every day on the special bus was another piece of proof. Every family argument was another brick in the wall. I was building a case for why I'd never amount to anything, and I was doing it unconsciously, gathering reasons to stay small without even realising that's what I was doing. After Uganda, I became someone who refused to accept that story. Not overnight - it wasn't a switch. It was more like someone lit a pilot light in my chest and everything after that was about feeding it fuel. I came back to Sheffield and the estate looked the same, smelled the same, but I was different. I started making decisions from a different place. Not from fear of ending up like the people around me, but from belief that I could build something they'd never imagined. That blind musician gave me something no teacher, no family member, no coach ever had - he gave me proof that limitation is a story, not a fact.

How often does this story come up in your work — and what impact does it have when you share it?

It comes up constantly because it's the root of everything I teach. When I'm telling a client that their current situation isn't their permanent reality, Uganda is in the background. When I'm pushing someone past their comfort zone and they're telling me all the reasons they can't, I'm thinking about a man who couldn't see and never let it stop him. When I share it publicly - on a podcast, in a talk, in content - the room changes. People go quiet in a different way. Not polite quiet. Processing quiet. Because it's not a business story or a strategy story - it's a human story, and it cuts past all the defences people put up when they think you're just trying to sell them something. It reminds people that the most powerful shifts don't happen in boardrooms. They happen in dusty villages, at kitchen sinks, on buses you didn't want to ride.

7. Describe your rock bottom. What were the circumstances, and what pulled you through?

My rock bottom wasn't a single moment - it was a season. The meal prep business had collapsed. Sixty grand a month to nothing. Thirty grand thrown at a marketing company that delivered sweet nothing. I was back to pot washing - hands in dirty water, scrubbing other people's plates in a kitchen that smelled of grease and bleach. I'd gone from being the boss to being invisible. But the real rock bottom wasn't the money or the status - it was the silence in my own head when I finally stopped running long enough to hear it. The voice that said "you're back where you started, and maybe that's where you belong."

What was the internal and external reality of that period — financially, emotionally, relationally?

Financially, I was counting coins. Not metaphorically - actually counting change to figure out what I could eat that day. I'd gone from buying whatever I wanted to checking my bank balance before buying a sandwich. Externally, I put on a front. Told people I was "figuring things out" and "taking a step back to reassess" - which is the polished version of "I failed spectacularly and I don't know how to fix it." Emotionally, I was hollowed out. I'd wake up and the first thought wasn't about what I was going to build - it was about what I'd lost. The relationships around me were strained because I was impossible to be around. I was angry, withdrawn, ashamed. I pushed people away because I couldn't stand them seeing me at the bottom when they'd watched me at what I thought was the top. The loneliest part wasn't being alone - it was being surrounded by people and feeling like none of them could see what was actually happening inside me.

Was there a specific moment where something shifted — a conversation, a realisation, a choice?

It wasn't dramatic. There was no cinematic turning point with music swelling in the background. It was a Tuesday. I was at the sink in that kitchen, pot washing, and I just stopped. Hands in the water, staring at nothing. And this thought landed - not gently, more like a brick through a window: "You've been here before and you climbed out. The only difference between then and now is you know more." That was it. Not a grand revelation. Just a simple recognition that I wasn't the same person who built the meal prep business. I was the person who'd built it, watched it fail, understood why it failed, and now had knowledge that most people pay six figures for. The shift wasn't emotional - it was structural. I stopped grieving what I'd lost and started auditing what I'd gained. Every mistake, every bad hire, every wasted pound, every sleepless night - it was all data. And data, when you organise it properly, becomes a system. That's when SmartScaling was born - not in a brainstorm session or a whiteboard moment, but at a kitchen sink with pruned fingers and a brain that finally stopped feeling sorry for itself.

What do you now know about yourself because of that experience that you couldn't have learned any other way?

I know I can survive the version of failure that most people only fear theoretically. That's not arrogance - it's just evidence. When you've actually lived through losing everything and rebuilding from a pot washing sink, the fear of failure loses its power over you. I also learned that I'm not my business. When the meal prep company collapsed, I thought I collapsed with it because I'd made my identity inseparable from what I'd built. Now I know that I'm the builder, not the building. Buildings fall down. Builders learn how to make the next one stronger. I learned that my brain - the ADHD, the ASD, all the things I thought were broken - is actually the reason I can see patterns other people miss, build systems faster than most, and hyperfocus on a problem until it's solved. The pot washing taught me humility. The rebuilding taught me structure. The failure taught me that the foundation is everything, and if you skip it, it doesn't matter how pretty the building looks.

8. What's a story from your childhood or early life that quietly runs in the background of everything you do today?

The special bus. Every single morning, from Base Green to Dronfield, I got on a bus that told the entire world I was different before I'd even opened my mouth. It wasn't a regular school bus - it was the one that picked up kids who needed extra support, kids who didn't fit the standard mould. And every morning, I watched the normal buses pass us. Normal kids, normal routes, normal lives. I'd press my forehead against the window and wonder what it felt like to just be regular. To not have a label before you'd even introduced yourself. That bus ride is in every single thing I build, every person I coach, every piece of content I create - because I know what it feels like to be told you're different before you've had a chance to show what different can do.

What was the environment, dynamic, or event — and how did young you interpret it?

The estate was rough in the way that council estates are rough - not dangerous every second, but heavy. There was a weight to it. Alcohol running through the family like a river nobody acknowledged. My mum, my dad, the adults around me - they were coping, not living. I interpreted it the only way a kid can: this is normal. This is what houses feel like. This is what families do. The bus was just another layer of that - another thing that confirmed I was on the outside of something everyone else seemed to be inside of. My brother did well at rugby and people knew his name. I had performance anxiety so bad I'd feel sick before games. Young me interpreted all of this as a single, consistent message: you're not enough. Not enough for your family to be sober, not enough to ride the normal bus, not enough to perform when it mattered. I carried that interpretation like a backpack full of rocks for years without realising I could just take it off.

How has that early experience shaped your values, your drive, or your fears as an adult?

It shaped everything. My obsession with systems comes from growing up in chaos - I build structures because I know what it feels like when there aren't any. My drive to help business owners comes from knowing what it's like to feel like you're the only one on the special bus, watching everyone else cruise past. My fear - and I still have it - is being put back in that box. Being labelled, dismissed, underestimated. Every time someone writes me off or assumes I'm not capable, that kid on the bus flares up and says "watch this." My directness comes from a household where nobody said the real thing. My belief in AI and systems comes from having a brain that couldn't operate like everyone else's, so I found tools that worked with my wiring instead of against it. Base Green didn't just give me a postcode. It gave me a blueprint for everything I refuse to accept and everything I'm determined to build.

If you could go back and speak to that younger version of you, what would you say?

I'd sit next to him on that bus, and I wouldn't say anything motivational. I wouldn't tell him it gets better or that one day he'd build a multi-seven-figure business. He wouldn't believe it and it wouldn't help. I'd just say, "that thing you think is wrong with you - it's not wrong. It's just different. And different is going to be the reason you win, not the reason you lose. The bus doesn't define you. The estate doesn't define you. The noise at home doesn't define you. One day you're going to stand in a village in Africa and a blind man is going to show you something that changes everything. Hold on until then." And then I'd probably just sit with him. Because what that kid needed more than words was someone who wasn't trying to fix him or change him - just someone who'd sit next to him on the bus and not make him feel like he was riding it alone.

9. Tell me about the biggest failure or mistake you've made in business or life — and what it actually taught you.

Scaling the meal prep business from eight grand to sixty grand a month with zero systems underneath it. It's like building a skyscraper on a sandcastle foundation and being surprised when the tide comes in. I was so obsessed with growth, so addicted to the numbers going up, that I never stopped to ask "can this thing actually hold the weight I'm putting on it?" The answer was no - spectacularly, expensively, humiliatingly no.

What decisions or behaviours led to it — and were there warning signs you ignored?

Every warning sign you can imagine - I saw them all and ignored every single one. Staff were overwhelmed and I just told them to work harder. Processes were held together with my personal involvement in every decision. The finances were messy but revenue was high so I told myself it was fine. I outsourced marketing to a company for thirty grand without doing proper due diligence because I was too busy running around like a headless chicken to sit down and evaluate what they were actually delivering. The behaviour underneath all of it was ego. Pure ego. I believed that my hustle, my energy, my ability to outwork any problem was enough. I treated business like a boxing match where you just throw more punches and eventually you win. But business isn't boxing. Business is engineering. And engineers don't skip the blueprints.

How did you respond in the immediate aftermath — did you run, face it, or freeze?

I froze first, then I ran. Not physically - I didn't disappear. But emotionally, I shut down. I went through the motions of wrapping up the business while feeling absolutely nothing. It was like watching a car crash from outside my own body. Then I ran into work - any work. Pot washing, kitchen jobs, whatever would stop me from sitting still long enough to feel the full weight of what had happened. I was terrified of stillness because stillness meant thinking, and thinking meant facing the fact that I'd done this to myself. It took months before I could actually face it properly. Sit down, look at what went wrong without blaming the market, the staff, the marketing company, the economy. Just me. My decisions. My lack of systems. My arrogance. That was the hardest conversation I've ever had, and it was with myself.

What do you now do differently as a direct result, and how does this make you a better coach or leader?

Everything I build now starts with the system, not the sale. Before I think about growing something, I ask "what does this look like at 10x volume and can it handle it?" I build the processes first, the frameworks first, the SOPs first - the unglamorous, boring, unsexy infrastructure that nobody posts about but everyone who succeeds has. When I work with clients, I'm practically allergic to vanity metrics. I don't care about your follower count or your launch revenue - I care about your delivery system, your client experience, your operational capacity. Because I've seen what happens when the numbers look great and the foundations are rotten. It makes me a better coach because I'm not teaching theory. I'm teaching from scar tissue. When I tell someone their business will collapse without systems, it's not a sales pitch - it's a weather report from someone who's already been through the storm.

10. What's the most unexpected plot twist your life has taken — something you never saw coming?

Getting diagnosed with ADHD and ASD in my late twenties. I'd spent my entire life thinking I was defective - wired wrong, built broken, fundamentally flawed in a way that everyone could see and nobody could fix. Then a professional sits across from me, runs through the assessment, and essentially says "you're not broken, your brain just works differently, and by the way, it's the reason you're good at half the things you're good at." Twenty-plus years of believing something was wrong with me, and it turns out the thing I thought was the problem was actually the engine.

What were your plans or expectations before this twist — and how did reality diverge from them?

I never had a plan for "what if my brain is actually fine." My entire operating system was built around compensating for what I assumed was broken. I'd developed workarounds, coping mechanisms, brute-force strategies to mask the fact that I couldn't function like a neurotypical person. My expectation was that I'd always have to work twice as hard just to keep up with people who found basic things effortless. Reality diverged because the diagnosis didn't just give me a label - it gave me a map. I use Monday.com to manage the ADHD. I use frameworks and routines to manage the ASD. And I use the unique way my brain fires to build things that people with "normal" brains genuinely can't.

It created someone who builds businesses the way his brain actually works instead of forcing his brain to work like a business textbook. Every system I design, every framework I teach, every tool I recommend - it's all filtered through the lens of someone who knows that the standard approach doesn't work for everyone. It's why I'm obsessed with AI and automation - because these tools do for other people what I spent years desperately needing: they take the chaotic, overwhelming, impossible-to-manage parts of running a business and turn them into something structured, predictable, and manageable. The diagnosis didn't change who I was. It just gave me permission to stop pretending I was someone else. What background, subculture, or life experience shaped this way of speaking?

Council estate Sheffield, rugby changing rooms, commercial kitchens, boxing gyms. These are environments where nobody speaks in frameworks or acronyms. The right audience - business owners doing £50K to £150K who've been in the trenches - hear my language and think "this is someone who's actually done it, not someone who's read about doing it." SECTION 6: Growth, Pressure & Self-Awareness

The weeks after the meal prep business collapsed. Externally I was applying for kitchen jobs, showing up on time, keeping my head down, doing the work. Internally I was in freefall - replaying every decision, calculating every pound I'd lost, lying awake at 3am with a brain that wouldn't stop running the numbers on a business that no longer existed. What default behaviours or coping mechanisms kick in — and which do you wish didn't?

Isolation and over-working. When the pressure rises, my ADHD brain locks into hyperfocus mode and I use work as the escape. If I'm busy enough, I don't have to feel it. If I'm solving problems, I don't have to sit with the discomfort of not having the answers. It's productive on the surface but it's avoidance wearing a hi-vis jacket. I also go to food - that's the old comfort pattern from growing up. A takeaway at midnight isn't a meal, it's a coping mechanism. I wish the isolation didn't kick in because the irony is that the people around me - Stacey, Rob, Ben, Kyran - are exactly the resource I need and exactly the resource I push away.

What work have you done to close the gap between who you are under pressure and who you want to be?

Working with Kyran at Total Mental Performance has been the single biggest shift. I've also built systems specifically for my ADHD-ASD brain using Monday.com, which means when my mental state drops, the structure holds. I don't have to think about what needs doing because the system already knows. That's prevention before invention applied to my own psychology. I'm not fixed - I don't think you ever are - but I catch it earlier now, and I ask for help before the mask is fully on.

32. How do you self-sabotage — and how do you catch it when it's happening?

I overcomplicate when I'm scared. When something important is on the line - a big launch, a new programme, a piece of content that actually matters - my brain starts adding layers of complexity that don't need to exist. Suddenly a simple sales page needs a complete rebrand. Suddenly a content plan needs twelve more posts. It's procrastination dressed up as perfectionism, and it's kept me stuck more times than I'd like to admit. The other pattern is comparison scrolling - I'll see someone in my space doing well and instead of using it as fuel, I'll use it as evidence that I'm behind.

What are the specific patterns, behaviours, or avoidance strategies you recognise in yourself?

Overcomplicating is the big one - adding unnecessary steps to delay the thing that actually moves the needle. Comparison scrolling, usually late at night when my defences are down. Over-preparing for things that need action, not preparation - I'll research and plan and strategise when what I actually need to do is press publish. And the sneaky one: helping other people with their stuff to avoid doing my own. I'll spend three hours on a client's problem because it's easier than facing the uncomfortable task on my own to-do list. It looks like generosity but it's avoidance.

What typically triggers these patterns — fear, comparison, success itself, something else?

Fear of it not being good enough. Which is really fear of ME not being good enough, wearing a different outfit. Every time I'm about to put something out that actually matters - something with my real story, my real face, my real opinion - the council estate kid shows up and says "who are you to do this?" The special bus lad who was told he was different, who watched his brother succeed at rugby while he froze with anxiety. That kid still lives in my operating system, and when the stakes get high enough, he starts running the show. Success itself triggers it too - I've noticed that after a big win, I'll unconsciously create a problem to solve because calm feels unfamiliar and chaos feels like home.

What does catching and calling out your own self-sabotage now look like in practice?

Kyran and I have built a system for it. When I notice the complexity creeping in, I literally stop and ask myself "is this moving the needle or is this moving the goalposts?" If I can't answer honestly, I call someone - usually Rob or Ben - and say "I think I'm overcomplicating this, tell me if I'm wrong." Nine times out of ten, they confirm what I already knew. The Monday.com system helps too because when I see a task that's been sitting untouched for three days while everything around it gets done, that's a signal. That untouched task is usually the one that matters most, which is exactly why I'm avoiding it.

Being dismissed. When someone talks over me, ignores my input, or treats my perspective as less-than - something in me goes from zero to a hundred in seconds. What story or wound sits underneath each trigger?

Being dismissed goes straight to the special bus. Twelve years of being treated as less-than because you learn differently wires you to react when anyone signals that you don't belong in the room. How have you learned to work with your triggers rather than be blindsided by them?

Sometimes that's taking a drink of water, sometimes it's literally saying "give me a second." It doesn't always work perfectly, and I still get caught off guard. But the difference between now and five years ago is the gap between trigger and response has gone from zero to just enough to choose who I want to be in that moment.

Trying to carry everything alone. It's the pattern underneath all the other patterns. I'll build a team and then not delegate properly. The lad who learned early that if you want something done right, you do it yourself - except that belief has a ceiling, and I've been hitting it repeatedly.

What has this pattern cost you, and how many times have you recognised it before acting?

The period between the business failing and hitting rock bottom.
